Eating Disorders

Anorexia

     Anorexia is an eating disorder where one has an immense fear of gaining weight, and has a misleading perception of weight. Approximately 1.2% of the total population of those 15 and older have anorexia at some point in time. Anorexia has led to many more deaths than several other eating disorders. Those with anorexia restrict the amount of food they consume, and they control their calorie intake by vomiting, misusing laxatives and diet aids etc. There is no exact cause of anorexia, but factors such as peer pressure, obsessive-compulsive personality traits, genetics, and anxiety could be potential causes. Some symptoms include exercising excessively, not eating in public, social withdrawal, insomnia, denial of hunger, only consuming safe foods etc. Those with anorexia could experience fatigue, dizziness, dry skin, a thin appearance, and dehydration.

Bulimia

     Bulimia is an eating disorder in which one binges or overeats food, and then uses either purging or nonpurging methods to restrict calories. Purging methods include vomiting, taking laxatives or other medicines to clear the intestines while nonpurging methods include excessive exercising and fasting. Like anorexia, there is no exact cause for bulimia, but some factors that cause it include genetics, society influence, etc. Some symptoms of bulimia are an above average body weight, peculiar eating habits, episodes of binge eating, excessive fasting and exercising, anxiety and depression. About 4.7 million or 1.5% of women in the US and 1.5 million or 0.5% males have experienced bulimia.

Pica

     Pica is an eating disorder that involves eating items that are not typically thought of as food and that do not contain significant nutritional value, such as hair, dirt, and paint chips. Pica often occurs with other mental health disorders associated with impaired functioning (e.g., intellectual disability, autism spectrum disorder, schizophrenia). Prader Willi Syndrome

     Prader Willi Syndrom is caused by the deletion of a part of chromosome 15, which is passed down by the father. Common symptoms include behavior problems, intellectual disability, short stature, delayed puberty, and constant hunger leading to obesity. There are less than 20,000 cases per year nationwide. Some symptoms are treated with hormone therapy and a supervised diet.
